Tomatoes are heavy feeders, which is a polite way of saying they'll strip your soil of nutrients fast if you don't replenish it. Most problems people blame on "bad soil" or "bad weather" actually trace back to a feeding schedule that doesn't match what tomatoes need at each stage of growth. Here's what actually matters when picking an organic fertilizer, and which types are worth your money.

What the numbers on the label mean

Every fertilizer lists three numbers, like 4-6-3. These are the percentages of Nitrogen (N), Phosphorus (P), and Potassium (K) by weight. For tomatoes specifically:

  • Nitrogen (N) drives leafy growth. Too much and you'll get a jungle of vines with barely any fruit.
  • Phosphorus (P) supports root development and flowering — this is the number to prioritize once flowers start forming.
  • Potassium (K) supports fruit development and overall plant resilience.

A good rule of thumb: lower nitrogen, higher phosphorus and potassium once your plants start flowering. A fertilizer around 3-4-6 or similar tends to work better at that stage than an all-purpose high-nitrogen blend.

Granular vs. liquid vs. compost-based

Granular organic fertilizers are slow-release — you work them into the soil and they feed the plant gradually over weeks. Good for a "set it and forget it" approach at planting time.

Liquid fertilizers (like fish emulsion or seaweed extract) act fast and are absorbed quickly, making them useful as a mid-season boost, especially right when flowering starts. The tradeoff is you need to reapply more often, generally every 2–3 weeks.

Compost and worm castings aren't a fast fix, but they improve the soil itself over time — better structure, better microbial life — which pays off across every future growing season, not just this one.

A simple feeding schedule

  1. At planting: work a granular organic fertilizer into the soil.
  2. At first flowers: switch to a phosphorus/potassium-leaning liquid feed every 2–3 weeks.
  3. Ongoing: top-dress with compost or worm castings once a season to keep building soil health long-term.

That's really it — the biggest mistake most first-time growers make isn't picking the "wrong" fertilizer, it's feeding the same blend the entire season regardless of what stage the plant is in.
